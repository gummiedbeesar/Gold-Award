My QuestBridge Journey

I learned about QuestBridge‚Äôs National College Match during my junior year. The College Match is a program where students can apply to up to 15 partnered schools for free and if they ‚Äúmatch‚Äù with (are accepted into) a school, they receive the QuestBridge Scholarship which covers housing and tuition for all four years (for the most part). In simple terms, applicants get a chance to receive a nearly full-ride scholarship to some of the top schools in the United States. Questbridge works as another form of Early Decision; during the process, you are not allowed to apply to other schools during their early rounds unless it meets one of the criteria listed on the QuestBridge website. For me, I applied early (not Early Decision) to the University of Memphis and the University of Tennessee - Knoxville because they fell into the allowed scenarios.

The QuestBridge process was very difficult and time-consuming, but well worth it. The first part of the application was the application for QuestBridge. This opened in early August and was due in late September. It included some typical college application questions and eleven open-response questions to get to know you better. The questions varied in word count with some being limited to 35 words while another was limited to 800 words. After completing this portion you receive the decision of whether you are a Finalist or not by mid-October.

If you don‚Äôt become a finalist, you are still able to apply to some of the schools partnered with QuestBridge with the QuestBridge Application. You are also allowed to apply to other schools Early Decision or Early Action. If you aren‚Äôt a Finalist, though, you may have to submit application fees.

I became a Finalist and moved on the the next portion of the National College Match Process: finalizing the order of my ranked schools and completing any required supplementals. When you become a finalist, the schools will reach out to you about accessing your portal. Then you have to complete EVERY supplemental for EVERY school you ranked. It is very important to note that when you are ranking your schools you need to think about a few things. Does the school have what I‚Äôm looking for? If you already know what you want to study and the school does not offer that, you should reconsider whether you want to rank them as a school. Do I want to go to the school? You should think about whether you actually want to attend the school. They are all great schools, don‚Äôt get me wrong, but not every school is perfect for everyone. If you Match with a school, you MUST attend, so really consider the previous two factors. And finally, will I be able to complete the supplementals needed? I ranked 6 schools: Northwestern University, The University of Chicago, Stanford University, the University of South California, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, and Columbia University in the City of New York. I had about 19 supplementals. Most were not more than 500 words, but the University of Chicago required an essay that was around 1-2 pages. The lengths and types of questions vary from school to school. The University of Southern California asked me quick questions like what my favorite snack was or what show I would binge next. You also need to complete the financial aid documents (the CSS Profile and FAFSA). This was all due by November 1st, the typical Early Decision deadline for schools. There was not much time between receiving the news that I was a finalist to submitting all the required materials, so I highly recommend looking at all the schools beforehand to see what they will ask for.

The next big date is December 1st, also known as Match Day. This is the day that you find out if you matched/who you matched with. When you rank schools, you are ranking them in the order of favorite to least favorite (of the schools you chose). The school you match with is the school that accepts you and is the highest on your list. Let‚Äôs say a student ranks 4 schools and they match with their 3rd choice. This means that the student was not accepted into their 1st or 2nd choice, but was accepted to their 3rd. This also means that the student will not know if they were accepted or rejected from their 4th choice. While yes, it‚Äôs possible School 4 accepted the student, because the student had School 3 ranked above School 4, they Match with School 3.

If you don't match, this is not the end of ANYTHING. You can roll your application over to the Regular Decision round and apply to schools without restrictions placed by QuestBridge. You also still have the title of ‚ÄúNational College Match Finalist‚Äù. I am very thankful to have Matched with my number one choice, Northwestern University! When I found out, I was elated! I can honestly say that the entire journey, from August to December, was SO worth it.

Even after Match Day, there is more to do. Most, if not all, schools will require you to keep up your academic record. Just because you Match does not mean your spot is unconditionally guaranteed. No matter how academic you are, senioritis is very real. Along with this, each school and QuestBridge will have some smaller things that need to be completed, so it is important to keep an eye out for those.

I know I‚Äôve been rambling on for a while now, but the final thing I want to say is, ‚ÄúYou got this.‚Äù This process is hard. It takes a lot of time, work, and energy. It‚Äôs going to feel like you are the only one stressed. It‚Äôs going to feel like you are behind. It‚Äôs going to feel like you aren‚Äôt ready. But none of that is true.
